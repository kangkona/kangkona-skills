# Lexicon-Inspired Public Research Notes

Use this reference when a user asks for the research basis, wants a Lexicon Branding-style process, or needs a deeper naming rationale. This is a distilled study of public materials, not an official Lexicon methodology or affiliation.

- Lexicon process article, June 26, 2026: David Placek describes a strategy-first process and names such as Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, CapCut, Sonos, BlackBerry, Impossible Foods, Pentium, and Swiffer.

## Case Patterns

### BlackBerry

Pattern: tactile metaphor plus friendly phonetics.

Public accounts describe the original device as a pager-like email product. The name used the keyboard's berry-like keys and a familiar fruit metaphor to soften a technical product. It avoided sterile category language while making the device feel approachable and memorable.

Reusable move: if the product is new or intimidating, use a concrete sensory metaphor that makes the behavior feel human.

### Pentium

Pattern: coined architecture name plus premium technical suffix.

Public accounts describe Intel needing a protectable successor to numeric chip names. `Pent-` suggested the fifth generation, while the ending made the name feel like a material, element, or technical platform rather than a mere model number.

Reusable move: when literal naming is legally weak or too generic, coin a compact name anchored in one real strategic fact.

### Swiffer

Pattern: action sound plus category behavior.

The name feels fast, light, sweeping, and slightly playful. Its sound carries the product's motion and convenience, which matters more than describing the cleaning mechanism.

Reusable move: for behavior-changing products, encode the action rhythm into the name.

### Febreze

Pattern: functional benefit plus sensory relief.

The name blends freshness/air associations with a breezy structure. It points to the after-state rather than the chemical mechanism.

Reusable move: name the desired atmosphere or emotional result, not the ingredient.

### Dasani

Pattern: invented premium refreshment.

Public profiles describe the name as selected from many candidates and shaped to feel relaxing and water-like without being a generic water descriptor.

Reusable move: for crowded consumer categories, use an invented name that creates sensory tone and leaves room for brand building.

### Azure

Pattern: familiar word, elevated semantic field.

Azure evokes sky, openness, and blue infrastructure without saying cloud or server. It works because it creates a larger mental space than a literal cloud-computing descriptor.

Reusable move: for platforms, pick a name with expansive semantic capacity.

### Sonos

Pattern: short, symmetrical, sound-adjacent coined name.

The name suggests sound while remaining broad enough for a home audio ecosystem. The mirrored structure helps memorability.

Reusable move: when the category is broad, use sound-adjacent cues instead of a literal product description.

### Impossible Foods

Pattern: strategic provocation.

The name frames ambition and category tension. It is not just a food descriptor; it announces a mission that invites curiosity and debate.

Reusable move: when the brand is built on a non-obvious breakthrough, let the name carry a bold claim.

### Vercel, Windsurf, CapCut

Pattern: modern software names with different strategic jobs.

Public Lexicon materials cite these as recent examples. Vercel feels technical and platform-like, Windsurf feels fluid and exploratory, and CapCut is direct and action-led for a consumer creation tool.

Reusable move: choose the level of abstraction based on audience and adoption path; developer platforms can tolerate coined precision, while consumer tools may need faster behavioral clarity.

## Distilled Principles

- Start with strategy, not wordplay.
- Define what a winning name needs to accomplish before judging candidates.
- Generate many names and expect most to fail.
- Use lanes to preserve diversity: descriptive, metaphoric, experiential, coined, compound, phonetic, and contrarian.
- Treat sound as meaning, not decoration.
- Prefer distinctiveness when category language is crowded.
- Use consumer/audience testing to learn perceptions, not to outsource taste.
- Treat legal and linguistic screening as part of the creative process, not a final clerical step.
- Present names in context with story, not as naked words on a slide.
- Do not confuse internal consensus with market memorability.

## Practical Prompts

Use these prompts inside the naming process:

- "What familiar category should this name escape?"
- "What new belief should this name make easier to hold?"
- "If the product expands 3x, does the name still fit?"
- "Does the name create a picture, motion, texture, or mood?"
- "What does the name sound like before people know what it means?"
- "Would a competitor be annoyed that we own this word?"
- "Is the name distinctive because it is strong, or just because it is weird?"
- "Can sales, press, and product each tell the name story in one sentence?"

## Presentation Heuristics

- Show fewer names with stronger rationale.
- Group names by strategic lane before asking stakeholders to react.
- Explain the tradeoff of each finalist: clarity vs distinctiveness, safety vs memorability, literalness vs expansion.
- Include one safe option, one bold option, and one balanced option in the top three.
- Make the reject list explicit so weak but tempting directions do not re-enter the process.
